Inside the FDA's Case Against Tavneos

On April 27, 2026, the FDA's Center for Drug Evaluation and Research (CDER) sent Amgen a formal Notice of Opportunity for a Hearing , the legal first step toward stripping Tavneos (avacopan) of its US approval. The letter is unusually detailed, laying out a granular, date-by-date account of exactly how two unblinded ChemoCentryx employees allegedly altered trial outcomes in November 2019. 01

Two Legal Grounds

The FDA isn't just questioning the science , it's invoking two specific provisions of the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act, either of which is independently sufficient to justify withdrawal.

Ground One

Lack of substantial evidence of effectiveness

New information shows the trial's primary endpoint results were manipulated after database lock. Once the manipulated data is set aside, the FDA says the original, valid analysis never actually showed the drug worked , meaning the legal bar for approval was never truly met.

§505(e)(3) FD&C Act · 21 CFR 314.150(a)(2)(iii)
Ground Two

Untrue statements of material fact

Separately , and more seriously , the FDA says ChemoCentryx's application itself contained false statements: about when and how many times the database was locked, and about what analyses were actually used to evaluate the trial.

§505(e)(5) FD&C Act · 21 CFR 314.150(a)(2)(iv)

The letter is blunt about why this matters beyond this one drug: "Withdrawing a drug under these circumstances is critical to protect the public health and the integrity of the drug approval process. Otherwise, applicants would be incentivized to deceive FDA and, if the deception is discovered by FDA after approval, claim the drug should remain on the market while the applicant gets another chance to reanalyze data and hope for a positive result."

03

What Actually Changed

Nine patients were selected for "readjudication" after the database was already locked and unblinded. Six were in the avacopan arm; five of those six flipped to "sustained remission." Only one of three control-arm patients changed at all, and , tellingly , none of the three control patients even had their week-52 status reassessed, since two had already left the trial early and the third's steroid use made a flip impossible either way.

Five of six avacopan-arm patients reviewed were reclassified into "sustained remission" , the trial's key measure of success. That swing was enough, on its own, to move the overall result from statistically non-significant (p=0.1025) to significant (p=0.0132). The FDA notes one of the nine , subject 466-003 , involved a genuine manual data-entry error; the other eight turned on debatable interpretations of missing-data and steroid-use rules that, per the FDA, should have been resolved before the database was ever locked, not after everyone already knew which patients got the real drug.

05

Why the FDA Says This Can't Be Fixed

Amgen's position is that the readjudications simply corrected genuine data errors. The FDA's letter directly rejects that framing, for reasons that come down to basic trial-design logic.

Blinding exists to prevent exactly this. The whole point of a double-blind trial is that nobody analyzing outcomes knows who got the real drug. The moment two ChemoCentryx staff saw unblinded data and then went looking for patients to reclassify, any subsequent "correction" was inherently compromised , deliberately or not.

The selection pattern gives it away. The FDA points out that twice as many avacopan patients were flagged for review as control patients (six versus three), every single patient chosen was one whose reclassification could only help the drug's numbers, and the three control patients selected couldn't have changed the outcome even if reclassified , two had already exited the trial early, and the third's steroid use blocked a "sustained remission" finding regardless. Tellingly, no one even bothered requesting a week-52 review for those three.

You can't re-test your way to the truth. Statistically, re-analyzing the same data repeatedly after seeing an unfavorable result inflates the odds of a false positive (Type I error) , precisely what pre-registered statistical analysis plans exist to prevent. The FDA states plainly that no further reanalysis, blinded or not, can rehabilitate this trial: the only valid analysis is the original one from November 5, 2019, and that one failed.

Even the paperwork forbade it. The trial's own Adjudication Committee Charter explicitly barred readjudication after database lock, stating "no changes may be entered into EDC after database lock" and "there will be no blinded BVAS listings for review after unblinding." The FDA's letter states directly that none of the nine readjudications were justified or permitted under that charter.

It fooled the agency's own decision-makers. The FDA reveals that its own primary review team did not recommend approval even without knowing about the manipulation , the drug was approved anyway because a division director believed (based on what turned out to be false representations) that the trial was a large, well-controlled, low-bias study. Had he known the true, non-significant original result, the letter states he would not have recommended approval.

06

How This Same Letter Reached the EMA

The FDA's Notice and the EMA's revocation recommendation aren't two separate scandals , they're two regulators responding to the exact same underlying facts, on their own legal tracks, a few weeks apart.

Same trigger, same evidence. The Walton Report that surfaced in the ChemoCentryx shareholder litigation in May 2025 is the root event for both agencies. Once the FDA's own investigation confirmed the report's core allegations , via Amgen's August 2025 response , the same underlying facts about the November 2019 database manipulation became the basis for the EMA's independent review, opened in January 2026. Neither agency had to do original detective work; both were working from the same reconstructed timeline of unblinded access, targeted patient selection, and the p-value flip from 0.1025 to 0.0132.

Different legal machinery, same conclusion. The FDA's letter is built around two specific US statutory grounds , lack of substantial evidence of effectiveness, and untrue statements of material fact under the FD&C Act. The EMA operates under EU pharmacovigilance law and its CHMP scientific review process, which doesn't use the same "untrue statement" framing. But the CHMP's own finding , that the ADVOCATE trial breached good clinical practice and that the submitted efficacy data were "inaccurate and misleading" , is substantively the same conclusion the FDA reaches in far more granular detail: the trial's headline result cannot be trusted because of what happened after unblinding.

The FDA's letter directly undercuts Amgen's EU defense too. Amgen's strategy at its own July 2026 FDA hearing rests on the Duke Clinical Research Institute's independent, blinded re-adjudication , essentially arguing the drug still works if you redo the analysis properly. But the FDA's letter pre-empts that exact argument on statistical grounds: it states that any further reanalysis, blinded or not, increases the risk of a false positive once the original prespecified analysis has already failed and been reworked. Because the EMA's CHMP reached its revocation recommendation without waiting for the Duke results, and because the same statistical objection applies equally in Brussels as in Washington, the FDA's reasoning here effectively forecloses the strongest rebuttal Amgen has in both jurisdictions.

Timing tells the story. The FDA formally proposed withdrawal on April 27, 2026 , the same letter broken down on this page. The EMA's CHMP followed with its own revocation recommendation on June 26, 2026, and the NEJM retraction landed just three days later. Three independent bodies , a US regulator, an EU scientific committee, and a medical journal's editorial board , converged on the same conclusion within about nine weeks of each other, all tracing back to the eleven days in November 2019 detailed above.

The through-line, in one sentence

The FDA's letter is the detailed evidentiary record; the EMA's revocation and the NEJM retraction are two separate institutions independently agreeing, on the strength of that same record, that the ADVOCATE trial's result cannot stand.

07

What Happens From Here

1
30 days from publication
Amgen must request a hearing

Under 21 CFR 314.200, ChemoCentryx (via Amgen) has 30 days from the Federal Register notice to file a written notice of participation and request for a hearing, or it forfeits the right to contest the withdrawal entirely.

2
60 days from publication
Amgen must show a genuine factual dispute

Amgen must submit the data, information, and analyses it's relying on. A request can't rest on bare denials , it must show a genuine, substantial issue of fact. If it doesn't, the FDA Commissioner can enter summary judgment and deny a hearing outright.

3
July 2026
The hearing itself

Amgen is expected to lean on the Duke Clinical Research Institute's independent, blinded re-adjudication to argue the drug's benefit holds up regardless. Given the FDA's position that any further reanalysis is statistically compromised, this is an uphill argument.

4
Until a final order
The drug stays on shelves

Tavneos remains legally marketable in the US throughout this process , it can only be pulled once ChemoCentryx either fails to request a hearing (an automatic waiver) or the Commissioner issues a final withdrawal order after the hearing process concludes.
